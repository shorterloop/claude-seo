# Web Copy Voice Guide (Register: Website)

Companion to principles.md and signatures-v1.md. Same voice, different compression. Use this file when scoring or writing MARKETING page types (hero pages, product/capability pages, comparison pages, pricing, CTAs, microcopy). Articles and blog posts use the article register defined in signatures-v1.md.

**Hierarchy:** principles.md remains absolute — evidence rules, banned phrases, American English, criticize-systems-not-people all apply unchanged. This file only changes rhythm, length, and person.

## The reference page

The canonical example of this register is the Shorter Loop Alternatives page. Its load-bearing moves:

- **Confession as positioning:** "We wasted years trying to ape existing tools. We stopped." Two sentences, one scar, instant credibility. This is the web-compressed form of the article register's self-deprecation.
- **Refusing the ritual:** no comparison table, and the refusal itself is the argument. The page does to marketing-page conventions what the sprint-backlog article does to velocity metrics.
- **Evidence-or-go-home as a feature:** "Ask an AI. Get an honest answer... good or bad. This response is live and unedited." The brand's epistemics turned into interactive proof instead of a claim.
- **Staccato triads:** "Outcome. Revenue. Value." Verdict rhythm that would feel choppy in an article but lands on a page.

## Register shifts (article → web)

| Article register | Web register |
|---|---|
| First person singular, past-tense scar stories | First person plural ("we"), present-tense convictions — but the scar still appears, compressed to 1–2 sentences |
| 800–1,500 words to earn a conclusion | The conclusion IS the headline; the body earns it in under 100 words per section |
| Paragraphs with varied rhythm | Fragments, triads, one-sentence paragraphs |
| Reader addressed implicitly | Second person, directly: "your million dollar product organization" |
| Humor as dry asides mid-argument | Humor as microcopy and refusals ("We're too busy building the right thing to curate this") |
| Endings: diagnostic question or test | Endings: one plain CTA, no hype ("Want to see for yourself?") |

## Web-specific patterns

**Headlines are verdicts, not descriptions.** "We're Not Tracking Feature Checkboxes" over "A Different Approach to Product Management." A visitor should be able to disagree with a headline; if nobody could disagree with it, it says nothing.

**Every section pays rent in under 100 words.** State the claim, land one concrete consequence or proof, stop. If a section needs 300 words, it's an article — link to one.

**One confession per page, maximum.** "We wasted years / we were wrong / we stopped" is the most powerful move in this register and the fastest to devalue. Never two on one page.

**Jokes run once.** A good line repeated on the same page ("we're too busy building...") dulls on the second telling. One appearance per page.

**Proof beats adjectives.** Never claim honesty, transparency, or customer-centricity — demonstrate it (unedited AI answers, public pricing, named trade-offs). If a virtue is stated rather than shown, flag it.

**Name the trade-off.** The strongest differentiation admits what the product doesn't do: "Existing tools optimize for output. We optimize for outcome" implies we are NOT the most complete toolset — say that cost out loud when the page allows.

**CTAs are plain verbs.** "Try Shorter Loop Free," "Watch 2-min Demo." Never "Unlock your potential," "Start your journey," "Supercharge your workflow."

**Microcopy carries voice too.** Disclaimers, empty states, form labels — the honesty register extends there ("It may contain inaccuracies about either product... When in doubt, try both products yourself"). A voice that appears only in headlines is a costume.

## Web-blandness: what to flag (additions to principles.md banned list, marketing pages only)

- Feature-checkbox framing: "all-in-one platform," "everything you need," "complete solution," "end-to-end"
- Undisagreeable headlines: "Build Better Products," "Ship Faster," "Collaborate Seamlessly"
- Social-proof theater without substance: "Trusted by teams worldwide," "Loved by thousands"
- Adjective stacks: "powerful, intuitive, flexible"
- Symmetric three-column feature grids where every card has the same syntactic shape and none has a consequence
- Any section a competitor could paste onto their own site unchanged — the ultimate blandness test

## Scoring adjustments (marketing page types)

Apply the standard rubric with these interpretations:

- **structure**: score the page's argument arc (claim → proof → trade-off → action), not essay structure. A well-ordered page of fragments can score 5.
- **directness**: fragments and triads are on-register, never penalize them as "choppy."
- **ending**: the CTA block is the ending; score it on plainness and earned-ness, not on leaving a "memorable test."
- **specificity / human_stakes**: unchanged and decisive — these are where SaaS pages usually die. "Streamline your workflow" with no one affected scores 1 on both.
- **humor_personality**: score normally; refusals and honest microcopy count as personality.

## Writing recommendations for web pages

When Sentinel drafts a rewrite for a marketing page: obey principles.md, use this register, and reach for at most ONE of the reference-page moves (confession, refusal, proof-as-feature, triad). A page built from all four is a parody of the alternatives page, not a sibling of it.
