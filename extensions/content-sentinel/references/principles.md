# Voice Principles (Durable Layer)

Pages are SCORED against this file. These principles are expected to remain stable for years. Do not add surface stylistic flourishes here — those belong in signatures-v{N}.md and are never used for scoring.

## Language

American English spelling and idiom (organize, realize, behavior, skeptical). Flag British spellings as divergence.

## Earned authority

Claims should come from concrete experience, specific observation, or visible reasoning. "I have seen teams do X" is earned; "teams often struggle with X" floating free of any evidence is not. Authority is shown, not asserted.

## Concrete consequences over abstraction

Process problems must connect to customers, trust, careers, morale, money, quality, or wasted effort. A passage that describes activity, features, or process in the abstract — with no one affected and nothing at stake — is off-voice regardless of how polished it reads.

## Confidence proportional to evidence

- Strong opinions are welcome; false certainty is not.
- "I have seen" for experience. "Research shows" only with a credible, citable source.
- Never present an observation as a validated predictor or statistical fact.
- Never invent numbers, research, quotations, customers, outcomes, or dialogue.
- Specific numbers used rhetorically (thresholds from experience) are acceptable when framed as experience, not as findings.

## Criticize systems before people

The target is ideas, incentives, rituals, and system design — not individuals, juniors, or customers. When the story permits, the author is part of the criticism.

## Nuance after provocation

A bold opening earns attention; the body must complicate it, not merely repeat it. False binaries get replaced with more useful distinctions ("plans are unreliable, but planning is essential"). Trade-offs are made visible, not hidden.

## Plain language

Short and medium sentences, varied rhythm, natural contractions, plain nouns and verbs over abstract management language. It should read aloud comfortably. Occasional fragments for emphasis. Mild profanity is acceptable when it earns its place.

## Endings sharper than summaries

A piece should end with a diagnostic question, a practical rule, a usable distinction, or a small experiment — never a recap or generic inspiration.

## Banned phrases and patterns

Flag any of the following as divergence:

"in today's fast-paced world", "the key takeaway", "it is important to remember", "game-changer", "unlock" (metaphorical), "leverage" (as vague verb), "empower", "navigate the complexities", "ever-evolving landscape", "rapidly evolving landscape", "embark on a journey", "journey of innovation", "at the end of the day", "paradigm shift", "best-in-class", "next-generation", "supercharge", "reimagine", "seamless collaboration", "single pane of glass", "drive transformation", "actionable insights" (unless the action is named), "customer-centric" (unless behavior proves it), "robust and scalable" (unless technically demonstrated), "north star" (unless literally relevant).

Also flag: generic inspirational conclusions; artificially symmetrical prose; tidy numbered listicles that arrive before the lesson is earned; every sentence polished, cautious, and emotionally neutral; diplomatic phrasing that avoids saying anything.

## What off-voice looks like (for the scorer)

A passage is probably diverging when it: sounds like a management consultancy; uses abstract nouns with no concrete consequence; treats a framework as inherently good or bad; hides the author's involvement in a failure; adds unsupported percentages; ends motivationally; or could have been written by anyone about any company.
